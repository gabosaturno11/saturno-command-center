Push-Up

Master the Foundation of Upper Body Strength

Technique Guide

1
Hand Placement
Hands slightly wider than shoulder width, fingers forward with palms flat on the ground or slightly gripping the floor.
2
Starting Position
Start in all fours with shoulders right over the wrists and extend both legs back.
3
Body Alignment
Feet and heels together, legs straight. Posteriorly tilt the pelvis by engaging the glutes and abs.
4
Upper Body Position
Chest neutral or slightly open. Arms fully straight with elbows slightly back.
5
Scapular Engagement
Scapulas are depressed and neutral at this point.
6
The Descent
Lean slightly forward to initiate the movement, bend the elbows, naturally retract the scapulas.
7
Bottom Position
Get the chest to the floor aiming to keep the forearms as vertical as possible in the bottom position.
8
The Push
Push back up to the initial position, maintaining body alignment throughout.

5 Mistakes to Avoid

1

Lack of Strength

Push ups might be a "beginner" exercise, but it does require a base level of pushing strength. Scale it to your level, find an elevation to be able to perform the reps correctly and lower it over time.

2

Short Range of Motion

Get the chest to the floor and lock the arms at the top on every single rep. Full range of motion is essential for maximum benefit.

3

Round/Arch Back

Keep that body line as stable as possible. Round too much and you won't even be able to get the chest to the floor. Arch too much and you are turning this into a purely tricep exercise.

4

Flared Elbows

While I do not consider flaring the elbows inherently dangerous, it is surely a more risky position for the shoulders. Keep them slightly tuck to your body as this will also be more transferable to advanced bodyweight skills.

5

Shrugged Shoulders

Shrugging up the shoulders is simply an inefficient and in most cases dangerous way to perform push ups. Keep them depressed for the most stable position to push from.

3 Key Variations

1. Hand Width Variations
You can go wide, less wide, regular, shoulder width, narrow, or extra narrow. All are valid options and doesn't make much of a difference in muscle activation as many people think. Begin with a version that feels more comfortable to you, then vary your grip periodically to keep training fun and linked to your goals.
2. Elbow Position Control
You have probably heard the cue, tuck your elbows in, but look at what happens if we do that on a wide push up. Not good. Just think of forearm perpendicular to the floor as the degree of tucking will depend on the width of your push ups.
3. Scapula Position
Fully protracting at the top is useful to develop strength in that range, however, it is definitely not necessary. If going for max reps it's also extremely ineffective to do so. I would use it in specific context such as training for the planche.
